<b>                                 SPHERE
</b>

                            by Stephen Hauser




              based on the novel, SPHERE, by Michael Crichton




                              March 5, 1996








<b>     EXT.  STREET IN GEORGETOWN -- MIDDLE OF THE NIGHT
</b>
     Fog hangs above the street, amongst the streetlamps.  Apartment
     buildings line the curb, it is very quiet.  In one building, six floors
     up, a bedroom light is on.  WE SEE the silhouette of Norman's WIFE
     looking out the window.

<b>                         WIFE (O.S.)
</b>               What kind of a crash was it?

<b>                         NORMAN (O.S.)
</b>               You seen my suitcase?

<b>                         WIFE (O.S.)
</b>               There's nothing on the news about a
               crash.  Did he say what airline?

<b>                         NORMAN (O.S.)
</b>               He didn't say much at all.  Honey --

<b>                         WIFE (O.S.)
</b>               In the closet.  I don't understand,
               Norman -- why would they call you?

<b>                         NORMAN (O.S.)
</b>               Don't be so supportive, honey.

<b>                         WIFE (O.S.)
</b>               Well, I can't imagine five years of
               unemployment --

<b>                         NORMAN (O.S.)
</b>               Four.  Four years --

<b>                         WIFE (O.S.)
</b>               Four years of unemployment would
               look too good on a resume.

<b>                         NORMAN (O.S.)
</b>               Keep watching the news.  Are you
               watching the news?

<b>                         WIFE (O.S.)
</b>               How are you getting to the airport?
               You need money for the bus?

     A tinted-window black SEDAN travels down the street, pulls up in front
     of the apartment building.  A U.S.  NAVY decal is on the door.

<b>                         WIFE (O.S.)
</b>               There's a U.S.  Navy car outside,
               Norman.

<b>                         NORMAN (O.S.)
</b>               What?

<b>                         WIFE (O.S.)
</b>               Was it a military crash?

<b>                         NORMAN (O.S.)
</b>               I don't know.

<b>                         WIFE (O.S.)
</b>               They never used to send a Navy car.

<b>     THE LOUD, HARD CHOPPING OF A HELICOPTER PROPELLER
</b>
<b>     EXT.  PACIFIC OCEAN -- DAY
</b>
     Blinding sunlight, as the helicopter WHIPS by, speeding above the
     Pacific Ocean.

<b>     INT.  HELICOPTER -- DAY
</b>
     Norman, 53, disheveled, unshaven, sits in the back.  He studies a
     pamphlet in his hand: "AIRCRASH SURVIVAL: HOW TO COPE".  The PILOT, in
     the cockpit, turns to him.

<b>                         PILOT
</b>               What kinda work you in, Dr. Johnson?

     Norman quickly stuffs the pamphlet into his dufflebag.

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               I'm in psychology.  Was.  Still am
               really, just been a while.
                    (beat)
               I treat aircrash survivors.

<b>                         PILOT
</b>               Oh, so you're that Dr. Johnson.

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               You've heard of me?

<b>                         PILOT
</b>               Well, everybody's been talking about
               that report you wrote.

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               What report?

<b>                         PILOT
</b>               Huh?

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               What report?

<b>                         PILOT
</b>               You mean you don't know?

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               Know what?

     The pilot glances back at him again.

<b>                         PILOT
</b>               Must be another Johnson then.  Been
               flying so many scientists out here
               -- can't keep everyone straight.

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               Who's everyone?

<b>                         PILOT
</b>               Physicists, mathematicians,
               geologists, you name it.

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               Geologists?  At a plane crash?

<b>                         PILOT
</b>               Strange, isn't it?  In the middle of
               the ocean no less.

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               What the hell would a geologist be
               doing at a plane crash?

     Norman looks out the window and sees a group of ships circling in the
     water.

<b>     EXT.  SHIP DECK -- DAY
</b>
     The helicopter descends down onto the helipad, wind gusting about.  A
     young, female OFFICER -- holding her hat to her head -- rushes to
     greet Norman, as he climbs out of the helicopter.

     She looks down crudely at his shotty, nylon dufflebag.

<b>                         OFFICER
</b>               Any other bags, Dr. Johnson?

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               Just that.  Careful, it's heavy.

     She lugs it over her shoulder, no problem.

<b>                         OFFICER
</b>               No other equipment?  Scientific
               instruments?

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               No, they didn't say --

<b>                         OFFICER
</b>               This way, sir.

     Norman follows the officer away from the helipad.

<b>     INT.  SHIP
</b>
     She leads Norman down a flight of stairs.

<b>                         OFFICER
</b>               Captain Barnes wants to see you
               right away, sir.

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               Captain who?

<b>                         OFFICER
</b>               Barnes.  He's very eager to meet you.
               Been calling us every half hour to
               see if you're arrived.

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               Really?

     As they march down a hallway, Norman looks down at his wrinkled suit,
     tries to smooth it out.

<b>                         OFFICER
</b>               By the way sir, I've read your
               report.  I think it's brilliant.

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               What report?

<b>                         OFFICER
</b>               You mean they haven't told you yet?

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               Told me what?

     She staightens up, like she might have said too much.

<b>                         OFFICER
</b>               This way, sir.

<b>     INT.  BARNES' OFFICE
</b>
     CAPTAIN HAROLD BARNES, 60s, immaculate, proud of who he is, talks on
     the phone.

<b>                         BARNES
</b>               How's he look?...  Well, we can't all
               be officers, can we?
                    (laughs)
               They tell me he's brilliant.

<b>     EXT.  BARNES' OFFICE
</b>
     Norman stands in front of the door.  The doorplate reads: PROJECT
     COMMANDER -- CAPTAIN HAROLD BARNES.  Norman straightens his big-knotted
     tie.  Deep breath.

<b>     INT.  BARNES' OFFICE
</b>
     As the door opens, Barnes is putting down the phone, rises from his
     desk, as Norman steps inside.

<b>                         BARNES
</b>               Dr. Norman Johnson.  Welcome aboard.

     They shake hands.

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               Thank you, Colonel.

<b>                         BARNES
</b>               Captain.

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               Captain.  Right, forgive me.

     Barnes looks at Norman, sizing him up.  An akward silence.

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               Well, thanks for the opportuninty
               here -- my wife appreciates it.

<b>                         BARNES
</b>               Don't thank me, Dr. Johnson.  You
               weren't my choice.  The Pentagon made
               me take you.

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>                    (smiling)
               The Pentagon?  I didn't know I had
               friends in Washington.

     Norman tries to laugh as Barnes ushers him to the door...

<b>                         BARNES
</b>               Come with me.  The team's already
               waiting.

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               What team?

<b>     INT.  HALLWAY
</b>
     Barnes leads Norman down the corridor, lined with OFFICERS and GUARDS.

<b>                         BARNES
</b>               What have you been told so far?

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               The usual.  Plane crash.  Survivers
               unknown.  Routine stuff really.

<b>                         BARNES
</b>               Anything else?

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               Nothing else.

     A GUARD unlocks a large, steel door.

<b>                         GUARD
</b>               Captain Barnes.  Dr. Johnson.

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               Hello.

     Norman looks at the guard as they proceed through the doorway,
     wondering how the guard knows his name.

<b>                         BARNES
</b>               You talk to any reporters?  Any
               press?

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               Press?  No, I haven't.

     The steel door shuts loudly behind them.

<b>                         BARNES
</b>               Good.  Security's been our biggest
               worry.  Now that you're here we can
               shut this thing down tight.

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               From what?  What's with all the
               security?

<b>                         BARNES
</b>               Well, we don't have all the facts
               yet.

     Another GUARD opens up another large steel door...

<b>     INT.  TECH ROOM
</b>
     Barnes leads Norman through the room, crammed with video monitors,
     screens, and grids.  Officers talk into radios.  Technicians work on
     computers.

<b>                         BARNES
</b>               We're moving fast considering the
               storm.

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               Storm?  What storm?

<b>                         BARNES
</b>               A cyclone's on it's way in.  I
               thought they would have told you on
               the phone.

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               They didn't tell me anything.

     Norman tries to keep pace...

<b>                         BARNES
</b>               We've had divers working around the
               clock.  Take a look at this...

     ANGLE ON a large VIDEO SCREEN -- shows a diver walking on the ocean
     floor, holding a bright artificial flashlight.

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               How deep is he?

<b>                         BARNES
</b>               A thousand feet.

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               A thousand?  An airplane crashes into
               a thousand feet of water -- I don't
               want to sound pessimistic here, but
               I assume there are no survivors.

<b>                         BARNES
</b>               Survivors?  No, I wouldn't think so.

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               Then why am I here?

<b>                         BARNES
</b>               What?

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               What do you need me for?

     ON THE VIDEO SCREEN -- the diver shines his light on a large, metallic
<b>     OBJECT.
</b>
<b>                         BARNES
</b>               What crashed wasn't an airplane, Dr.
               Johnson.  It's a bit larger than
               that.

     Barnes walks toward a TECHNICIAN, sitting at a keyboard.

<b>                         BARNES
</b>                    (to technician)
               Bring up the grid, would you?

     ANGLE ON THE MONITOR as an IMAGE OF THE CRAFT begins to form.

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               What is it?  A military spacecraft?
               Like a shuttle or satellite?

<b>                         BARNES
</b>               Something like that.
                    (beat)
               That doesn't surprise you?

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               Not really, no.  Something of the
               military crashes in the ocean -- it
               explains why there was nothing on
               the news, why you've kept everything
               a secret...  When did it crash?

<b>                         BARNES
</b>               As best we can estimate, it crashed
               four hundred years ago.

     A beat.

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               Four hundred?  You're kidding, right?

     ANGLE ON THE MONITOR -- showing a grid depicting an image of the craft
     and a small layer of ROCK above it.

<b>                         BARNES
</b>               See this?  Coral.  Geologists measured
               the coral growth on top of the craft
               to be over five meters thick.

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               Geologists?

<b>                         BARNES
</b>               Coral grows at a rate of two and a
               half centimeters a year --

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               -- Wait a second --

<b>                         BARNES
</b>               -- dating the crash at least that
               old.

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               Hold on --

<b>                         BARNES
</b>               Maybe older --

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               -- there's got to be some mistake
               here -- a four hundred year old
               military spacecraft?  There's no such
               thing... our space program isn't even
               forty years old.

<b>                         BARNES
</b>               Extraordinary, isn't it?

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               It's impossible.

<b>                         BARNES
</b>               I'm afraid it is Dr. Johnson...
               because it's sitting on the bottom
               of our ocean floor.

<b>     INT.  STAIRWELL
</b>
     Barnes and Norman shuffle down the stairs.

<b>                         BARNES
</b>               Off the record, I don't mind telling
               you, this thing scares the shit out
               of me.

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               It doesn't make any sense.

<b>                         BARNES
</b>               We think it might.  That's why we
               brought you here.  We've assembled
               your team -- they're waiting for us
               now.

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               What team?

<b>                         BARNES
</b>               The one you recommended.  In the ULF
               report you wrote for the Bush
               administration.

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               ULF report?  Nobody's mentioned that
               in years.  You mean someone actually
               read it?

     As they head down a hallway, Barnes pulls the ULF REPORT out from
     under his arm, hands it to Norman.

     Norman looks at it, almost embarrassed to be holding it.

<b>                         BARNES
</b>               Your report's become our bible down
               here, Dr. Johnson.  We've been
               carrying out each of your
               recommendations -- one by one -- to
               the smallest detail.

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               But sir, I don't understand.  This
               report -- these recommendations...
               they're for an encounter with extra-
               terrestrial life.

<b>     INT.  BRIEFING ROOM
</b>
     ON A VIDEO SCREEN -- sonar video of the spacecraft.

<b>                         BETH (O.S.)
</b>               How big is this spacecraft?

<b>                         BARNES (O.S.)
</b>               Of what we're able to measure,
               roughly twenty-six hundred feet.

<b>                         TED (O.S.)
</b>               A half a mile long?

<b>                         BARNES
</b>               The wing dimension alone is larger
               that any aircraft we've seen.

     The team members sit around the conference table:

     TED FIELDING, 41, geologist, boyish and happy to be here.  BETH
     HALPERIN, 36, biochemist, mother of three who's never worn an apron.
     ARTHUR LEVINE, 54, marine biologist, pudgy and quiet.  HARRY ADAMS, 32,
     mathamatician, African-American, wire-glasses, no bullshit.

<b>                         HARRY
</b>               How much damage?

<b>                         BARNES
</b>               To the craft?  None.  Not a scratch.

<b>                         HARRY
</b>               Right.  So you're saying it survived
               a high speed impact with water
               without a scratch?

<b>                         BARNES
</b>               I'm not saying anything, Harry, I'm
               just stating the facts.
                    (continuing)
               The outer metal is made of a
               titanium alloy, built into an epoxy-
               resin honeycomb.  The necessary
               technology to bond this type of
               metal has never been invented.

<b>                         TED
</b>               Never invented?

     ON NORMAN, watching the team...

<b>                         BETH
</b>               In basic English, what does all this
               mean?

<b>                         BARNES
</b>               Basic English?  There's no way this
               spacecraft was constructed on our
               planet.

<b>     A LOUD MECHANICAL WHIRR
</b>
<b>     INT.  TESTING ROOM -- LATER
</b>
     A large, X-Ray APPARATUS moves to the side, revealing Norman's FACE.

     ON BETH -- standing next to him.

<b>                         BETH
</b>               Five years.  No phone calls.  What am
               I -- a leper?

     Norman sits up...

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               Beth, look, can I trust you with a

<b>                         BETH
</b>               I love secrets.

               oxygen tanks.

<b>                         BETH
</b>               Well, you must have realized the
               chances an encounter like this would
               happen here.  In the ocean.
                    (off his look)

               water, Norman.  That's first grade
               geography.

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               It never occurred to me.

<b>                         BETH
</b>               Well, let's pray that's the only
               mistake you made.  Have you said
               anything to Barnes about this?

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               The timing has never seemed quite
               right.

<b>                         BETH
</b>               Just keep your mouth shut, alright?
               This could mean everything to our
               careers, you know that?  Don't
               jeopardize this.

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>                    (nervous laughter)

               it?

<b>                         ARTHUR
</b>               It scares the shit out of me.

<b>                         BARNES (O.S.)

<b>                         BARNES (O.S.)

<b>                         BARNES (O.S.)

</b>               Arthur?

<b>                         NORMAN

<b>                         ARTHUR
</b>               Open the hatch door, please.

     Harry continues to whisper in his ear.

<b>                         ARTHUR

     The sub LURCHES again, coming to a stop.  The compartment is totally

     And Arthur, wiping the vomit from his mouth, climbs up the ladder and

</b>               Anybody else?

     The hatch door LOCKS, loudly.  The sub lurches, and begins descending.

</b>               Pussy.

<b>                                                            CUT TO:
</b>
<b>     INT.  DECOMPRESSION ROOM
</b>
     A pale, white room.

     ON NORMAN -- sleeping.  His eyes begin to open.  He awakes.

     BETH -- is next to him, looking at him.  There is a long beat.

<b>                         BETH
</b>               Thank you.

     Norman looks up at her... 

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               You know, I think you really need to
               see a psychologist.

     She stares back at him, and smiles.  Begins laughing with him.

<b>     EXT.  DECOMPRESSION CHAMBER
</b>
     TWO NAVY CREWMEN stand outside the door, looking in.

<b>                         CREWMAN #1
</b>               Has anyone spoken to them yet?

<b>                         CREWMAN #2
</b>                    (shakes his head)
               The Admiral's going over the tapes
               they brought up now.

<b>                         CREWMAN #1
</b>               Does he know yet what really
               happened down there?

     Looking INSIDE -- Norman, Harry, and Beth sit at a table.

<b>     INT.  DECOMPRESSION CHAMBER
</b>
     At the table... 

<b>                         HARRY
</b>               They're going to want answers and
               they're going to keep asking until
               they get them.

<b>                         BETH
</b>               We can refuse to talk.

<b>                         HARRY
</b>               Won't make any difference.  The tapes
               document everything.

     A long beat.

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               There is one possibility.

<b>                         HARRY
</b>               We all kill ourselves?
                    (laughing, then)
               Bad joke.  Sorry, Beth.

     She smiles.

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               The power allows us to manifest our
               fears, right?  What if we feared the
               power to go away?

<b>                         HARRY
</b>               You mean forget?

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               It would erase all our knowledge of
               it.  All of our power.

<b>                         BETH
</b>               Would it work?

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               Yes.  And I think we already know it
               will.

<b>                         BETH
</b>               How's that?

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               Down below, Harry said we would all
               have to die.  Since there was no
               evidence on that spacecraft that
               anybody knew time travel through a
               black hole was possible -- and we
               all knew -- then we would all have
               to die before we could tell anyone.
               Death was the only logical
               explanation.  But sitting here, we
               still know.  So how is that possible?
                    (beat)
               Unless we make ourselves forget.

<b>     INT.  SHIP'S HALLWAY -- LATER
</b>
     Norman, Harry, and Beth walk with the CREWMEN.

<b>     INT.  ADMIRAL'S OFFICE
</b>
     The ADMIRAL, 60s, many stripes on his sleeves, sits behind his desk.
     Harry and Beth sit in front of him.  The TAPES on the desk.

<b>                         ADMIRAL
</b>               There are some questions that we
               need to address here.

<b>                         HARRY
</b>               What about?

<b>                         ADMIRAL
</b>               When you were being transferred to
               decompression, Dr. Adams, you
               mentioned something about a squid.

<b>                         HARRY
</b>               A what?

<b>                         ADMIRAL
</b>               A squid.  However, there doesn't seem
               to be any squid recorded on these
               tapes.

<b>                         BETH
</b>               A squid?  I don't remember anything
               about a squid.
                    (to Harry)
               Did you say something about a squid,
               Harry?

     Harry shakes his head.

<b>     EXT.  SHIP DECK -- DAY
</b>
     A bright, sunny day.

     ON THE HELIPAD -- the helicopter's propeller begins whirling.

<b>     INT.  HELICOPTER
</b>
     An OFFICER sits in the passenger's seat, glancing at a clipboard.

<b>                         OFFICER
</b>               You all ready to go, Dr... ?

     The PILOT from the opening scene... 

<b>                         PILOT
</b>               Johnson.  The psychologist.  The one
               that wrote the report.

     ON NORMAN -- in the back.

<b>                         NORMAN
</b>               What report?

<b>                                                            CUT TO:
</b>
<b>     BLACK.
